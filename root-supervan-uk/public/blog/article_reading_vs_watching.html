Productivity Hack: Reading vs. Watching for Developers

Ask a Senior Developer how they learn a new framework, and they will likely point to the documentation. Ask a Junior Developer, and they might send you a YouTube tutorial. Why the difference?

The Bandwidth of Text

The average human speaks at about 150 words per minute. The average adult reads at 250 words per minute. A skilled skimmer (like most developers scanning for code snippets) can process 500+ words per minute. Video is inherently throttled by the speed of speech. Even at 2x speed, video is often slower than reading for information retrieval.

Random Access Memory (RAM) vs. Serial access

Text provides "Random Access." You can look at a page, jump to the bottom, glance at a code block, and read the first sentence of a paragraph. You are in control of the flow. Video is "Serial Access." You must proceed linearly (mostly) to ensure you don't miss context. Scrubbing a video bar is imprecise compared to Ctrl+F on a webpage.

The Copy-Paste Friction

For developers, the inability to copy code directly from a video frame is a massive friction point. It forces context switching—looking at the video, typing, looking back. OCR (Optical Character Recognition) tools help, but they are clunky. Text-based tutorials allow for instant transfer of syntax to IDE.

Why Video Still Wins (Sometimes)

This isn't to say video is useless. Video excels at high-level architectural concepts, visual workflows (like configuring AWS consoles), and "soft skills" or reasoning. Establishing the *mental model* is often easier when hearing an expert explain their thought process. But for the "how-to," text reigns supreme.
